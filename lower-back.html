Core Stabilizers
(Transverse Abdominis, Multifidus, Obliques) 		Weak deep core muscles reduce spinal stability, forcing superficial muscles and the lumbar spine to overwork. This compensation increases load on the spine, contributing to lower back pain (Weak core muscles can cause lower back pain | 220 Triathlon). A weak core often causes the lower back to overextend on foot strike. Uphill running still requires core engagement to maintain posture, so core weakness can lead to faster back fatigue. Side Plank Test – Lie on your side supported by one forearm and feet, keeping your spine straight. Hold as long as possible. Inability to hold ~60 seconds (or a >5% time difference between sides) indicates core weakness or imbalance (Back pain with running | Dynamic Health | Chiropractor Jersey CI).
(Back pain that eases uphill but worsens downhill is also a sign of poor core endurance (Dynamic Health).) 		Core Strengthening Exercises – e.g. Forearm Plank (trains transverse abdominis by keeping a straight back) (6 Core Exercises for Runners that Prevent Running Injuries) and Side Plank (emphasizes obliques and TA) (6 Core Exercises for Runners that Prevent Running Injuries). These stabilize the spine. Other exercises include bird-dogs, dead bugs, and abdominal bracing, which improve deep core activation and endurance.
Gluteal Muscles
(Gluteus Maximus, Medius, Minimus) 		The glutes stabilize the pelvis and extend the hip. Weak or inactive glutes (especially glute medius) cause pelvic drop and excessive trunk side-bending, leading to strain on the lumbar spine (6 Core Exercises for Runners that Prevent Running Injuries). During uphill running, demand on glute max is higher (The ups & downs of hill running - RunningPhysio). If the glutes don’t carry their load, the lower back and hamstrings compensate, contributing to fatigue and pain. Tight glutes can also tilt the pelvis and stress the low back (How underactive gluteal muscles can cause lower back pain | Lifemark). Single-Leg Bridge Test – Lie on your back, lift hips into a bridge with one foot on the floor and the other leg extended. Inability to keep the hips up (pelvis dropping) or feeling the hamstring cramp on the supporting side indicates glute weakness/dominance issues (Self-Assessing Gluteal Function — Garrett McLaughlin | Kalamazoo, MI). A positive Trendelenburg sign (hip drops when standing on one leg) also signals weak glute medius. Glute Strengthening & Activation – Clamshells, Glute Bridges, Lateral Band Walks/Sidesteps, and even side planks all target the gluteus medius and maximus (Fix Your Lower Back Pain by Strengthening the Gluteus Medius Muscle - Fox Physical Therapy). These exercises improve pelvic stability. Emphasize squeezing the glutes during hip extension movements. For tight glutes, incorporate stretching (figure-4 piriformis stretch) and foam rolling to restore flexibility.
Hip Flexors
(Iliopsoas, Rectus Femoris, etc.) 		Tight hip flexors (often from prolonged sitting) pull the pelvis into an anterior tilt and increase the arch in the lower back (Lower back pain in runners due to tight hip flexors - Physio and Fitness Clinic). This posture is exaggerated when running upright, and especially uphill (which requires more hip flexion). The result is more stress on the lumbar facets and quicker fatigue of lower back muscles. Weak or fatigued hip flexors can also alter running form, but tightness is a more common contributor to lower back issues (How underactive gluteal muscles can cause lower back pain | Lifemark). Thomas Test (Self-check) – Sit at the edge of a sturdy table and lie back hugging one knee to your chest. Let the other leg hang down. If the hanging thigh can’t drop parallel to the floor (hip remains flexed), your iliopsoas is tight; if the knee of that leg can’t bend ~90°, the rectus femoris (quadriceps) is tight (Tight hip flexors: How to diagnose yourself). This simple screen helps distinguish which hip flexors are limiting your mobility. Hip Flexor Stretches & Mobility – Regularly perform a Half-Kneeling Hip Flexor Stretch (kneel on one knee, tuck the pelvis under/posterior tilt, and shift forward) to lengthen the iliopsoas (Four Hip Flexor Stretches to Relieve Tightness, from a PT | HSS). Other PT-recommended stretches include lunging hip flexor stretches, dynamic leg swings, and the prone "runner’s stretch". Strengthening the glutes and core will also alleviate overreliance on hip flexors (Four Hip Flexor Stretches to Relieve Tightness, from a PT | HSS), and improving hip extension mobility ensures uphill running doesn’t excessively strain the low back.
Hamstrings
(Biceps Femoris, Semitendinosus, Semimembranosus) 		The hamstrings assist the glutes in propelling uphill. Uphill running forces the hamstrings (and glutes) to work in a shortened range, so they fatigue quickly (Running Injury Spotlight: Hamstrings — The Run Rx) if not strong or conditioned, potentially leaving the lower back to pick up the slack. Tight hamstrings are a common contributor to low back pain (Specific Hamstring Stretches for Back Pain Relief | Spine-health): they pull on the pelvis and limit pelvic mobility, increasing strain on the lumbar region. Hamstring Flexibility Test – A simple gauge is the classic Sit-and-Reach Test, which measures hamstring and lower-back flexibility (Sit and Reach Test to Measure Lower Back Flexibility). From a seated position with legs straight, reaching short of your feet or feeling intense stretch behind the thighs indicates tight hamstrings. An Active Straight Leg Raise (<80° hip flexion before the knee bends) also signifies reduced hamstring length (Top 5 Fridays! 5 Ways to Measure Hamstring Flexibility). Hamstring Stretches & Strength – Gentle hamstring stretches (standing toe-touch stretch, seated forward bend, lying towel stretch, etc.) done regularly will gradually lengthen the muscles and reduce stress on the lower back (Specific Hamstring Stretches for Back Pain Relief | Spine-health). For example, a standing hamstring stretch or a doorway stretch can improve flexibility. Ensure proper form (hinge at the hips, not the lower back). In addition, eccentric strengthening (e.g. Nordic hamstring curls or slow RDLs) can bolster hamstring endurance for uphill running, so they don’t fatigue as easily. Maintaining hamstring flexibility and strength helps keep the pelvis neutral and unloads the lumbar spine.
